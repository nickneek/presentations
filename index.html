The Question

In 2026, humanity can actually ask machines philosophical questions. AI has had breakthroughs across domains—protein folding, quantum error correction, gluon scattering amplitudes.

But philosophy does not have uncontroversial success conditions. What counts as a contribution depends on what philosophy is.

Two Conceptions of Philosophy

Text-Focused

Philosophy is located in texts. A contribution is a text exhibiting certain properties: coherence, explanatory power, integration.

Dellsen et al. · Bengson et al. · Williamson

Practitioner-Focused

Philosophy is located in the practitioner. It requires self-transformation, therapy, or phenomenological observation.

Hadot · Wittgenstein · Merleau-Ponty

This Paper’s Framework

If philosophical evaluation concerns properties of texts—coherence, handling of objections, illumination of subject matter—then whether LLMs can do philosophy is a question about the texts they produce.

We argue LLMs can produce philosophy exhibiting the relevant properties.

1

Philosophy in the Text

Discovery vs. Argument

Watson & Crick (1953)

Reported a finding. The double helix existed before they described it. A different scientist would have found the same thing.

Quine (1951)

Made arguments. There is no prior reality the paper reports. A different philosopher reaching the same conclusions would have had to make arguments.

If the arguments differed, so would the contribution.

Likeliness & Loveliness

Lipton distinguishes the likeliest explanation (most probable) from the loveliest (deepest understanding if true).

We can assess loveliness independently of whether an explanation is correct. Philosophical evaluation has this character: we assess arguments for properties judgeable from the arguments themselves.

What We Evaluate

  • Elegance and unity—a good theory explains much with little
  • Non-ad-hocness—parts hang together, not gerrymandered to cases
  • Handling of objections—distinctions drawn at the right places
  • Illumination—does it deepen understanding of the subject matter?

Blind review operationalises this: referees assess arguments without knowing who wrote them.

Deep Blue plays good chess. Its moves respond effectively to threats, secure positional advantages, and contribute to coherent strategic plans. But Deep Blue does not play creatively. A move is good chess or it is not, regardless of whether it was found by creative insight or brute computation.

If evaluation concerns properties of arguments, the production process is not evaluatively relevant.

2

LLMs and Abduction

Zeroth-Order Abduction

Floridi, Nobre & Taddeo (2024)

LLMs generate plausible continuations based on learned associations. They do not compare hypotheses and select the best. Floridi et al. call this zeroth-order abduction—the absence of genuine comparative evaluation.

"The model does not understand what an explanation is, but it produces text that follows the typical phrasing and structure of explanations." Floridi, Nobre & Taddeo

Manipulative Abduction

Zahavy (2026)

Einstein imagined himself inside a falling elevator. He simulated the sensations—objects hovering, the floor rushing up—and abduced that gravity and acceleration must be the same phenomenon.

LLMs operate entirely in symbols—"high-dimensional Chinese Rooms." But the argument extends beyond physics: if LLMs lack subjective experience altogether, then philosophy relying on phenomenological observation, intuitions (Machery 2017), or aesthetic experience is also at risk.

Thought Experiments &
Armchair Abduction

Armchair Abduction

Williamson (2024)

"It should be elegant and unified, not arbitrary, gerrymandered, ad hoc, or messily complicated. It should be informative and general. In brief, it should combine simplicity with strength." Williamson 2024, p. 354

The evidence for philosophical abduction is the accumulated textual record of the discipline. Mathematics is precedent: a successful armchair discipline where abduction operates.

P

The Corpus Argument

The philosophical corpus has been filtered by peer review—a process that selects for exactly the intrinsic virtues Williamson identifies.

An LLM trained on this corpus has learned the distribution. What counts as "plausible" in philosophy is what scores well on Williamson's virtues; and what scores well is what the corpus encodes.

Floridi et al.'s diagnosis—that LLMs produce plausible outputs—is mechanistically correct. But philosophical plausibility is philosophical quality.

Twin Earth Is a Text

Putnam's Twin Earth, Jackson's Mary, Searle's Chinese Room, Parfit's teleporter—every major philosophical thought experiment is articulated in language, recorded in text, and does its work at the level of concepts and propositions.

They do not require embodied simulation. Whatever private experiences philosophers have in arriving at them, the thought experiments themselves are linguistic objects.

Descriptions, Not Experience

We do not claim LLMs have phenomenological experience, intuitions, or aesthetic response. What they have is a training corpus containing extensive descriptions of human experience.

This is not first-hand access to experience but access to descriptions—and descriptions of experience are what philosophical argument typically works with.

Conceptual Innovation

Philosophical novelty consists in new ways of carving the problem-space:

  • Dummett—assertoric content vs. ingredient sense
  • Kripke—rigid designators reconfigure reference, identity, modality
  • Lewis—modal realism takes possible-world semantics literally

These are conceptual innovations—not leaps from bodily sensation to formal axioms. An LLM trained on the materials and the standards has the resources to produce similar reconfigurations.

"Arguing that Inference to the Best Explanation is wrong because Bayesianism is right is like arguing that thinking about technique cannot help my squash game because the motion of the ball is governed by the laws of mechanics." Lipton 2004, p. 108

The stochastic description of the LLM does not settle whether its outputs meet philosophical standards.

∴

Conclusion

If philosophical evaluation concerns properties of arguments—and these properties are assessable by reading them—then the production process is not evaluatively relevant.

The question is whether a text exhibits these properties, not what brought it into existence.
